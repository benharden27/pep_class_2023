Temperature, Salinity and Density

Some of the most basic properties of the ocean are it’s temperature (how hot/cold it is), Salinity (how salty it is), and density (how buoyant it is). As we will see, temperature and salinity dictate the density of the ocean and it’s the density that is a large driver or inhibitor of ocean movement.

Phytoplankton, Nutrients and Light

Phytoplankton are the base of many food webs in the ocean. They are single-celled plant-like organisms that typically get their energy from sunlight. They exists throughout the world’s oceans, primarily in the upper sunlit portion, but there are many factors that affect their abundance and geographic distribution.

Oxygen and Carbon

The ocean contains many elements, compounds and gases. Studying these and their interactions is the domain of chemical oceanography. This is clearly a very diverse field, but here, we will just touch on the basics of oxygen and carbon in the ocean. We wil look at both how they get into the ocean and then how they are moved from the surface to the deeper ocean by biology.

Plankton

Plankton are the community of drifters in the ocean. We’ve already met phytoplankton who produce their energy through photosynthesis, but now we’ll add zooplankton who primarily get their energy from consuming other plants and animals. The diversity of plankton is extremely large so we wont try and cover everything or everyone, but instead familiarize ourselves with some basic types and processes.

  • Chapter 12: pgs 305 - 314: The section on Plankton covers many basic catergories and processes.

One important process in the world’s oceans is Diel Vertical Migration (DVM), where the motile plankton community travel upwards in the water column at night to feed before sinking to deeper waters during the day to hide from predators.
